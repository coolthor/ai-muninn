[Part 1](/en/blog/part1-why-your-dgx-spark-says-exclamation-marks) was about getting Qwen3.5-122B to produce anything other than `!!!!!`. Four bugs, SM121-specific, all fixable. After patching them, the model boots clean and generates coherent output.

Then you check the speed.

**14 tok/s.**

This post is about why that number is where it is, and why there's nothing to tune.

---

## What I Wanted

The goal was simple: use Qwen3.5-122B-A12B-NVFP4 as the primary model on the DGX Spark. 122B parameters, NVFP4 quantization, 128 GB unified memory — on paper, the GB10 should handle this. The arithmetic works out: 122B × 4-bit ≈ 61 GB, leaving room for KV cache at the 128 GB ceiling.

With gpt-oss-120B hitting [59 tok/s](/en/blog/part2-gpt-oss-120b-serve-script), the expectation for 122B NVFP4 was in the same range. Maybe a bit slower — larger model, slightly different architecture. Not 14 tok/s.

---

## What I Tried

After the Part 1 fixes (SM121 PTX path, Marlin thread race, SupportsQuant, CUTLASS_FP4 exclusion), the model loads and runs. The output is correct. The first few tokens arrive fast. Then the throughput number stabilizes.

Tried the obvious things:

- **`--enforce-eager` removed** — wasn't there, not the issue
- **KV cache dtype** — fp8, same as gpt-oss, no difference
- **`--max-num-batched-tokens`** — tuned up and down, marginal effect
- **`VLLM_MXFP4_BACKEND=marlin`** — already set
- **`--moe-backend marlin`** — already set
- **GPU memory utilization** — 0.90, standard

Nothing moved the number meaningfully. 14 tok/s stayed 14 tok/s regardless of what was tuned.

---

## Where the Problem Actually Is

Qwen3.5-122B-A12B is a **hybrid architecture**. It is not a standard Transformer. It combines two types of layers:

- **Standard attention layers** — normal Transformer MHA/MLA, Marlin handles these fine
- **GDN (Gated Delta Network) layers** — SSM-style recurrent layers, must stay in BF16

The GDN layers are the issue. Marlin has specialized kernels for standard linear layers and MoE expert GEMMs. It does **not** have a specialized kernel for GDN. Every GDN GEMM falls back to a generic compute path.

On the GB10 (273 GB/s memory bandwidth), the generic fallback is slow enough that it dominates decode time. The attention layers run at normal speed; the GDN layers crawl. The throughput you observe is the harmonic mean of "fast Marlin path" and "slow generic path" — and 14 tok/s is what that average looks like with 122B parameters.

This is why the `SupportsQuant` fix from Part 1 matters: without it, the GDN layers get quantized to NVFP4, their recurrent state corrupts, and the output becomes garbage. The fix keeps them in BF16 — which is correct, but it means GDN runs on the non-specialized path.

**The bandwidth math:** At 273 GB/s, moving 61 GB of weights once takes ~0.22 seconds. Theoretical decode ceiling for 122B NVFP4 on GB10 is around ~30 tok/s. To hit that, every layer needs an efficient kernel. When half the architecture falls back to a generic path, you get roughly half the ceiling — which is close to where 14 tok/s lands.

---

## Conclusion

Qwen3.5-122B runs correctly on SM121 after the Part 1 fixes. Output is coherent. The model works.

14 tok/s is not a configuration problem. It is a software gap: Marlin does not yet have a specialized kernel for GDN layers. When that kernel lands — either in Marlin or in a future vLLM/Triton path — the throughput should recover toward the theoretical ~30 tok/s ceiling.

For now, if you need interactive speed on a DGX Spark: use gpt-oss-120B at 59 tok/s. Qwen3.5-122B at 14 tok/s is correct but not fast enough for interactive use.

If you came here because your Qwen is slow: you're not missing a flag. You've already found the ceiling.
